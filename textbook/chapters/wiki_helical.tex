\section{Helical Antennas}\label{sec:wiki_helical}

\input{pedagogy/wiki_helical_obj}

The helical antenna is a wire wound in a helix and fed at one end against a ground plane. Depending on the circumference-to-wavelength ratio it can operate in two distinct modes. The \textbf{axial mode} (end-fire) is the most useful for gain antennas — it produces a circularly polarised beam along the helix axis and is widely used in satellite ground stations, CubeSats, and amateur radio.

				

\subsection{Normal Mode vs Axial Mode}

				
\begin{itemize}

					\item \textbf{Normal mode}: circumference C ≪ λ; radiation broadside (perpendicular to axis); linearly polarised; low gain. Used as a compact antenna when space is limited.

					\item \textbf{Axial mode}: circumference C ≈ λ; radiation along the axis; circularly polarised; high gain. This is the practical gain antenna.

				
\end{itemize}

				

\subsection{Axial-Mode Condition}

				

The helix operates in axial mode when:

				
\[0.75 < \dfrac{C}{\lambda} < 1.33 \quad \text{where } C = \pi D\]

				

The optimal pitch angle (angle of the helix wire relative to the horizontal plane) is approximately α ≈ 14°, which corresponds to a turn spacing S ≈ λ/4 when C ≈ λ.

				

\subsection{Gain and Beamwidth (Kraus Formulas)}

				
\[G \approx 15\,N\!\left(\frac{C}{\lambda}\right)^{\!2}\!\frac{S}{\lambda} \quad \text{(linear, not dB)}\]

				
\[\text{HPBW} \approx \dfrac{52°}{\dfrac{C}{\lambda}\sqrt{N\dfrac{S}{\lambda}}}\]

				

where N is the number of turns. Gain increases with N — a 10-turn helix at C = λ, S = λ/4 gives G ≈ 15×10×1×0.25 = 37.5 (≈ 15.7 dBi).

				

\subsection{Input Impedance}

				

The input impedance of the axial-mode helix is nearly resistive and approximated by:

				
\[Z_{\rm in} \approx 140\,\dfrac{C}{\lambda}\;\;[\Omega]\]

				

At the optimum C/λ = 1, Zin ≈ 140 Ω. A quarter-wave stripline transformer between the helix feed and the 50 Ω coax connector matches the impedance: the transformer impedance is Z\_t = √(140 × 50) ≈ 84 Ω.

\subsection{Circular Polarisation}

				

The axial-mode helix produces circular polarisation naturally. The sense (RHCP or LHCP) is determined by the winding direction:

				
\begin{itemize}

					\item \textbf{Right-hand winding} (thumb along axis, fingers curl in direction of travel) → RHCP

					\item \textbf{Left-hand winding} → LHCP

				
\end{itemize}

				

For satellite reception (LEO downlinks, GPS), RHCP is standard. The axial ratio of a well-designed axial-mode helix is typically better than 1 dB (very close to perfect circular polarisation) over the 3 dB beamwidth.

				

\subsection{Design Procedure}

				
\begin{enumerate}

					\item Choose target frequency f → compute λ = c/f

					\item Set D = λ/π (so C = λ) and S = λ/4 for optimal pitch angle

					\item Choose N for desired gain: G ≈ 15N×1×0.25 = 3.75N → e.g. N = 10 gives G ≈ 37.5 (15.7 dBi)

					\item Compute Zin ≈ 140 Ω; design λ/4 transformer to 50 Ω (Z\_t ≈ 84 Ω stripline)

					\item Ground plane diameter should be at least 0.75λ to 1λ

				
\end{enumerate}
