\chapter{Coaxial Cable}\label{ch:wiki_coax_cable}

\input{pedagogy/wiki_coax_cable_obj}

Coaxial cable is the primary transmission line for RF signals above a few MHz. Choosing the wrong cable — even for a short run — can add significant insertion loss, degrade shielding, or limit power handling. This page is a quick reference for the most common types.

\section{Attenuation Scaling with Frequency}

      

Coaxial cable attenuation increases approximately as √f (due to skin effect in the conductors) plus a smaller f-dependent term from dielectric loss:

\rffig{wiki_coax_cable_1.pdf}{Coaxial cable attenuation increases as √f. LMR-400 has 5× less loss than RG-174 at the same frequency.}

      
\[\alpha \approx \alpha_1\sqrt{f} + \alpha_2 f \quad [\text{dB/m}]\]

      

At 100 MHz, LMR-400 has \textasciitilde{}0.044 dB/m. At 2.4 GHz it's \textasciitilde{}0.22 dB/m. A 10-metre run at 2.4 GHz loses 2.2 dB — worth using LMR-600 or a tower-mounted LNA for long runs.

      

\section{Velocity Factor and Electrical Length}

      

The velocity factor (VF) is the ratio of signal propagation speed in the cable to the speed of light. VF depends on the dielectric: solid PE ≈ 0.66, foam PE ≈ 0.78–0.88, PTFE ≈ 0.69. Electrical length = physical length / VF × 360° per wavelength. A 1-metre RG-58 cable at 300 MHz is one electrical wavelength long (0.66 × 1 m / 1 m = 0.66λ — 237°).

      

\section{Power Handling Limits}

      

Two limits apply: \textbf{thermal} (conductor heating → insulation damage, typically rated at 40°C ambient) and \textbf{voltage breakdown} (at peak voltage, the dielectric arcs over). For most cables the thermal limit is lower. Power derating at high temperatures: reduce rated power by \textasciitilde{}1\% per °C above 40°C. At altitude (lower air pressure), the breakdown voltage decreases — critical for airborne equipment.

      

\section{Practical Selection Guide}

      
\begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{0–30 MHz, any run length}: RG-213 or LMR-400. Loss is not critical.

        \item \textbf{VHF/UHF, \textless{} 3 m}: RG-58 or LMR-195. Adequate loss, easy to handle.

        \item \textbf{VHF/UHF, \textgreater{} 10 m}: LMR-400 or LMR-600 to keep feedline loss below 1–2 dB.

        \item \textbf{Microwave, \textgreater{} 2 GHz, critical loss budget}: LMR-400 or semi-rigid.

        \item \textbf{Pigtails and internal connections}: RG-316 or UT-085 semi-rigid.

        \item \textbf{High power (\textgreater{} 500 W)}: LMR-600 or larger hardline; ensure good connector torque.

      
\end{itemize}
