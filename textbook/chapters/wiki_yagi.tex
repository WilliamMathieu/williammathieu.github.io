\chapter{Yagi-Uda Antennas}\label{ch:wiki_yagi}

\input{pedagogy/wiki_yagi_obj}

The Yagi-Uda antenna (commonly called a Yagi) is a directional antenna composed of a fed dipole, a reflector behind it, and one or more directors in front. Only the driven element is connected to the feed line — the others are parasitic and couple electromagnetically. It is the dominant design for VHF/UHF terrestrial and amateur radio links and satellite ground stations.

				

\section{Element Roles}

				
\begin{itemize}

					\item \textbf{Reflector}: one element, positioned \textasciitilde{}0.25λ behind the driven element. Slightly longer than λ/2 (≈ 0.505λ). Reflects energy forward, increasing F/B ratio.

					\item \textbf{Driven element}: the only fed element, ≈ 0.47–0.48λ long. Input impedance in the array is \textasciitilde{}25 Ω (straight dipole) or \textasciitilde{}200 Ω (folded dipole).

					\item \textbf{Directors}: shorter than λ/2 (≈ 0.43–0.46λ), spaced 0.25–0.42λ forward of the driven element. Each director adds roughly 1–2 dBd of gain. Gain saturates above \textasciitilde{}9–10 elements.

				
\end{itemize}

dBi = dBd + 2.15. Adding directors past \textasciitilde{}9–10 elements yields diminishing returns (\textless{}0.5 dB per element) for significant boom length cost.

				

\section{Feed Impedance and Matching}

				

The driven-element feed impedance is reduced from the free-space dipole value (\textasciitilde{}73 Ω) by the mutual coupling of adjacent elements:

				
\begin{itemize}

					\item \textbf{Straight dipole}: Zin ≈ 25–35 Ω in the array — requires a matching network (gamma match, beta match) to 50 Ω coax

					\item \textbf{Folded dipole}: naturally 4× higher impedance → Zin ≈ 200–300 Ω → use a 4:1 balun or λ/4 transformer to 50 Ω

				
\end{itemize}

				

\section{Bandwidth and Scaling}

				

A well-optimised Yagi has an operating bandwidth of approximately 2–5\% of the centre frequency — narrow enough that element length must be scaled accurately. To move a design to a new frequency, scale all dimensions by the ratio of the original wavelength to the new wavelength (f\_old/f\_new).
