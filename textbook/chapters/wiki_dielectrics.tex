\section{Dielectrics and Permittivity}\label{sec:wiki_dielectrics}

\input{pedagogy/wiki_dielectrics_obj}

A dielectric is an insulating material that, when placed in an electric field, stores energy by polarising its molecules. The key RF properties of a dielectric are its relative permittivity (dielectric constant) and loss tangent.

				

\subsection{Relative Permittivity ($\varepsilon_r$)}

				

The dielectric constant $\varepsilon_r$ relates the permittivity of the material to that of free space: $\varepsilon = \varepsilon_r\varepsilon_0$. It determines how much wavelengths are shortened in the material: $\lambda = \lambda_0/\sqrt{\varepsilon_r}$.

\rffig{wiki_dielectrics_1.pdf}{PCB substrate map. FR4 is cheapest but lossy; Rogers and PTFE offer much lower tan δ for microwave applications.}

				

\subsection{Loss Tangent ($\tan\delta$)}

				

A real dielectric also dissipates energy. The loss tangent represents the ratio of the imaginary to real part of the complex permittivity:

				
\[\tan\delta = \dfrac{\varepsilon_r''}{\varepsilon_r'}\]

				

Lower $\tan\delta$ means lower dielectric loss. Attenuation in a transmission line increases with loss tangent and frequency.

				

\subsection{Common PCB Substrate Materials}

				

\rffig{wiki_dielectrics_2.pdf}{Microstrip cross-section. Field lines extend partly into air above the trace, giving εeff \textless{} εr.}

				
\begin{itemize}

					\item FR4 — $\varepsilon_r \approx 4.4$, $\tan\delta \approx 0.02$. Standard PCB material, adequate to \textasciitilde{}5 GHz.

					\item Rogers 4003C — $\varepsilon_r = 3.55$, $\tan\delta = 0.0027$. Low loss, stable with temperature, suitable to 77 GHz.

					\item PTFE (Teflon) — $\varepsilon_r \approx 2.1$, \textbackslash{}(\textbackslash{}tan\textbackslash{}delta \textless{} 0.001\textbackslash{}). Excellent high-frequency performance, used in coaxial cable dielectrics.

					\item Alumina (Al₂O₃) — $\varepsilon_r \approx 9.8$, $\tan\delta \approx 0.001$. Used in MMIC substrates.

				
\end{itemize}

				

\subsection{Frequency Dependence}

				

Both $\varepsilon_r$ and $\tan\delta$ vary with frequency. For PCB design above 1 GHz, always use substrate data measured at the operating frequency rather than the low-frequency value printed on datasheets.
