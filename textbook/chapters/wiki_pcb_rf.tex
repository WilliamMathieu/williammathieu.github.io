\chapter{RF PCB Design}\label{ch:wiki_pcb_rf}

\input{pedagogy/wiki_pcb_rf_obj}

At RF frequencies, PCB traces behave as transmission lines. Their characteristic impedance must be controlled to prevent reflections, signal integrity problems, and radiation. The four standard controlled-impedance structures are microstrip, stripline, coplanar waveguide (CPW), and differential pairs. Each has different shielding characteristics, loss properties, and PCB layer requirements.

\section{Coplanar Waveguide (CPW)}

      

CPW places the signal trace between two coplanar ground planes. No ground plane is needed below the substrate, making it ideal for flip-chip and MMIC die attachment where ground connections must be made on the surface. The characteristic impedance depends on the trace width \emph{w} and gap \emph{s} to the ground planes:

\rffig{wiki_pcb_rf_1.pdf}{Cross-sections of the four standard PCB RF transmission line types.}

      
\[Z_0 = \dfrac{30\pi}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_{eff}}}\dfrac{K(k^\prime)}{K(k)}, \quad k = \dfrac{w}{w+2s}\]

      

where K(k) is the complete elliptic integral of the first kind. Grounded CPW (GCPW) adds a bottom ground plane, suppressing the parasitic microstrip mode and is preferred for multilayer PCBs.

      

\section{Differential Pair Impedance}

      

Edge-coupled microstrip differential pairs carry balanced signals. The key parameter is the differential impedance $Z_{diff} = 2 Z_{odd}$, where $Z_{odd}$ is the odd-mode impedance. Standard targets: 100 Ω differential for USB/HDMI/LVDS, 90 Ω for PCIe.

      
\[Z_{diff} = 2Z_{odd} \approx 2Z_0\left(1 - 0.347e^{-2.655 s/h}\right)\]

      

where \emph{s} is the edge-to-edge spacing and \emph{h} is the substrate height. The even-mode impedance $Z_{even} \approx 2Z_0/(1 + 0.347e^{-2.655 s/h})$ determines common-mode behaviour. Tight spacing reduces Z\_diff; looser spacing increases it toward 2×Z₀\_single.

      

\section{Stripline}

      

Stripline is a flat conductor centred symmetrically between two ground planes. It is a TEM mode structure with no dispersion and no radiation — unlike microstrip whose fields extend into air above the trace. The impedance:

      
\[Z_0 = \dfrac{60}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_r}}\ln\!\left(\dfrac{4b}{0.67\pi w_{eff}(0.8+t/w)}\right)\]

      

where \emph{b} is the ground-plane separation and $w_{eff}$ accounts for trace thickness. Because the medium is uniform, $v_p = c/\sqrt{\varepsilon_r}$ with no effective permittivity correction.

      

\section{Via Parasitics}

      

A PCB via connecting signal layers has inductance and capacitance:

\rffig{wiki_pcb_rf_2.pdf}{Via cross-section showing inner conductor, antipad, and the parasitic inductance + capacitance equivalent circuit.}

      
\[L_{via} \approx 0.2h\ln\!\left(\dfrac{4h}{d}\right)\text{ nH}, \quad C_{via} \approx \dfrac{1.41\varepsilon_r h}{\ln(D/d)}\text{ pF}\]

      

where \emph{h} is the via height (mm), \emph{d} the drill diameter (mm), and \emph{D} the antipad diameter (mm). At 1 GHz, a standard FR4 via (0.3 mm drill, 1.6 mm deep) has about 0.5 nH inductance and 0.15 pF capacitance — enough to cause significant reflections at microwave frequencies. Via stubs (unused portions below the signal layer) are particularly problematic and are removed by backdrilling.

      

\section{Bond Wire Inductance}

      

Gold or aluminium bond wires connecting a die to a package or PCB have parasitic inductance (Grover formula):

      
\[L_{wire} = 0.2\ell\!\left[\ln\!\left(\dfrac{2\ell}{d}\right) - 0.75\right]\text{ nH}\]

      

where ℓ and d are in mm. A typical 1.5 mm gold bond wire (d = 25 µm) has about 1.2 nH inductance, giving 7.5 Ω reactance at 1 GHz — often requiring resonating capacitors in MMIC matching networks.
