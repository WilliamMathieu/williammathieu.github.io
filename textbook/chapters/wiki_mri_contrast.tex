\chapter{MRI Contrast and Relaxation}\label{ch:wiki_mri_contrast}

\input{pedagogy/wiki_mri_contrast_obj}

MRI contrast arises from differences in tissue relaxation times T1 and T2, proton density (PD), and flow. By choosing appropriate pulse sequence parameters, specific tissue properties are emphasized, allowing discrimination of normal from pathological tissue.

				

\section{T1 Relaxation (Spin-Lattice)}

				

\rffig{wiki_mri_contrast_1.pdf}{T1 recovery (purple) and T2 decay (orange) curves. T1 ≥ T2 always. Different tissues have different values.}

				

T1 is the time constant for recovery of longitudinal magnetisation after excitation. Energy is transferred from the spin system to the surrounding lattice (molecular framework). T1 reflects molecular mobility: water in CSF has long T1 (\textasciitilde{}4 s at 3 T); fat has short T1 (\textasciitilde{}260 ms) due to efficient energy transfer at the Larmor frequency. Short TR → T1-weighted image.

\rffig{wiki_mri_contrast_2.pdf}{T1: longitudinal magnetisation recovers exponentially toward M₀. T2: transverse magnetisation decays. Tissue contrast comes from T1/T2 differences.}

				

\section{T2 Relaxation (Spin-Spin)}

				

T2 is the time constant for decay of transverse magnetisation. Local field inhomogeneities from neighbouring spins cause dephasing. T2 is always ≤ T1. Fluids have long T2 (\textasciitilde{}2 s for CSF) appearing bright on T2-weighted images; solid tissues have short T2. Long TE → T2-weighted image.

				

\section{T2* and Susceptibility}

				

T2* includes additional dephasing from macroscopic B0 field inhomogeneities (susceptibility differences, main magnet imperfections). T2* \textless{} T2. Gradient echo sequences are T2*-weighted. Susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI) exploits T2* to visualise iron deposits, venous blood, and calcifications.

				

\section{Contrast Agents}

				

Gadolinium-based contrast agents (GBCAs) shorten T1 in their vicinity, making enhancing tissue appear bright on T1-weighted images. Used to detect blood-brain barrier breakdown, tumour vascularity, and inflammation. Iron oxide nanoparticles shorten T2/T2*, creating signal voids.
