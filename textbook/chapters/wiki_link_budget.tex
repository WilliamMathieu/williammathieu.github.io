\section{Link Budgets}\label{sec:wiki_link_budget}

\input{pedagogy/wiki_link_budget_obj}

A link budget is a systematic accounting of all gains and losses in a communication system from transmitter to receiver. It determines whether a link will achieve adequate signal-to-noise ratio under specified conditions, and by how much margin.

				

\subsection{Link Budget Equation}

				

\noindent\hspace*{1.2em}\textit{$P_r = P_t + G_t - L_{tx} - FSPL - L_{misc} + G_r - L_{rx}$   (dBm)}

				

Then compare received power to the minimum detectable signal (MDS):

\rffig{wiki_link_budget_1.pdf}{Link budget bar chart: TX power flows through gains and losses. Green margin bar = working link.}

				

\noindent\hspace*{1.2em}\textit{$MDS = kTB + NF + SNR_{min}$   (dBm)}

				

Link margin = $P_r - MDS$. A positive margin means the link works; typical design margins are 3–20 dB depending on the fade margin required.

				

\subsection{Free-Space Path Loss}

				

\rffig{wiki_link_budget_2.pdf}{Link budget chain: each block contributes gains (antennas) or losses (cables, path loss). The sum determines received power vs noise floor.}

				
\[FSPL = 20\log_{10}(d) + 20\log_{10}(f) + 20\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{4\pi}{c}\right)\]

				

At 2.4 GHz, FSPL over 100 m ≈ 80 dB. FSPL grows with distance² and frequency² — doubling distance adds 6 dB; doubling frequency adds 6 dB.

				

\subsection{Additional Loss Mechanisms}

				
\begin{itemize}

					\item \textbf{Multipath fading} — reflections cause constructive/destructive interference. Fast fading margin: 20–30 dB for 99.9\% link reliability.

					\item \textbf{Rain attenuation} — significant above 10 GHz; up to several dB/km in heavy rain.

					\item \textbf{Atmospheric absorption} — oxygen absorption peak at 60 GHz (15 dB/km) limits this band to short-range applications.

					\item \textbf{Polarisation mismatch} — misaligned transmit/receive polarisations cause 0–∞ dB loss.

				
\end{itemize}
