\chapter{Thermal Management and Junction Temperature}\label{ch:wiki_thermal}

\input{pedagogy/wiki_thermal_obj}

An RF power amplifier turns much of its DC power into heat, and that heat has to escape from a tiny transistor die to the outside world. The temperature the die actually reaches — the \emph{junction temperature} $T_j$ — governs both performance (gain, efficiency and output power all fade as the device heats) and reliability (failure rate rises steeply with temperature). Thermal design is therefore inseparable from PA design.

      

\section{The Thermal-Resistance Chain}

      

Heat flows from junction to ambient through a series of thermal resistances, exactly like current through series electrical resistors, with power as the "current" and temperature rise as the "voltage":

\rffig{wiki_thermal_1.pdf}{The junction-to-ambient thermal chain: die → case → heat sink → air, each stage a thermal resistance in °C/W.}

      
\[\theta_{ja} = \theta_{jc} + \theta_{cs} + \theta_{sa},\qquad T_j = T_a + P_{diss}\,\theta_{ja}\]

      

Here $\theta_{jc}$ (junction-to-case) is fixed by the device and package, $\theta_{cs}$ (case-to-sink) is the interface — thermal grease, a pad, or solder — and $\theta_{sa}$ (sink-to-ambient) is the heat sink and airflow, the part the designer most controls. The dissipated power $P_{diss}$ is the DC input minus the RF output: a 40 \% efficient 10 W PA dissipates 15 W.

      

\section{Maximum Power and Margin}

      

Every device has a maximum rated $T_{j,max}$ (often 150–200 °C for silicon, higher for GaN). Turning the equation around gives the most power the thermal path can carry, and the margin at a given operating point:

      
\[P_{max} = \dfrac{T_{j,max}-T_a}{\theta_{ja}},\qquad \text{margin} = T_{j,max}-T_j\]

      

Good practice is to derate — design for a junction well below the rating (e.g. $T_j\le$ 125 °C) to buy reliability and headroom for a hot ambient. Note the strong lever of ambient temperature: the same amplifier that is fine on the bench at 25 °C can exceed its rating in a sealed enclosure at 70 °C.

      

\section{Temperature and Reliability}

      

Semiconductor failure mechanisms (electromigration, diffusion) accelerate roughly with an Arrhenius law — a common rule of thumb is that failure rate doubles for every \textasciitilde{}10 °C rise in junction temperature. Keeping $T_j$ down is therefore the single biggest lever on the mean-time-between-failures of a power stage. High temperature also shifts bias points and lowers gain and P1dB, so thermal and electrical performance must be co-designed.

      

\section{Practical Techniques}

      
\begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Minimise interface resistance}: thin, high-conductivity thermal interface material, flat mating surfaces, adequate mounting pressure.

        \item \textbf{Spread the heat}: thermal vias under the die pad, copper coins or heat spreaders, direct die-attach to the flange.

        \item \textbf{Size the sink}: choose $\theta_{sa}$ (finned extrusion + airflow) for the required $P_{max}$; forced air or liquid cooling for high power.

        \item \textbf{Watch duty cycle}: pulsed operation uses \emph{transient} thermal impedance, which is lower than the steady-state value for short pulses — a pulsed PA can run higher peak power than its CW rating.

      
\end{itemize}
