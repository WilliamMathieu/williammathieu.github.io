\begin{workedexample}[Worked Example: Skin depth in copper at 1\,GHz]
The skin depth is
\[ \delta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi f \mu \sigma}}, \]
with copper conductivity $\sigma = 5.8\times10^{7}\ \mathrm{S/m}$ and
$\mu = \mu_0 = 4\pi\times10^{-7}\ \mathrm{H/m}$. At $f = 1\ \mathrm{GHz}$,
\[ \pi f \mu \sigma = (\pi)(10^{9})(1.2566\times10^{-6})(5.8\times10^{7}) = 2.29\times10^{11}, \]
\[ \delta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2.29\times10^{11}}} = \frac{1}{4.78\times10^{5}} = 2.09\times10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 2.1\ \mu\mathrm{m}. \]
Almost all of the RF current is packed into the outer couple of microns of the
conductor --- which is why plating quality and surface roughness dominate loss at
microwave frequencies.
\end{workedexample}

\begin{keytakeaways}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item $\delta = 1/\sqrt{\pi f \mu \sigma}$; skin depth falls as $1/\sqrt{f}$.
\item About $63\%$ ($1-e^{-1}$) of the current flows within one skin depth of the surface.
\item AC resistance therefore rises as $\sqrt{f}$; this favours large-surface or
silver-plated conductors and makes surface finish critical.
\end{itemize}
\end{keytakeaways}

\begin{exercises}
\item Copper's skin depth is $2.1\ \mu\mathrm{m}$ at $1\ \mathrm{GHz}$. What is it at $100\ \mathrm{MHz}$?
\item If the operating frequency increases by a factor of $100$, how does the skin depth change?
\item Gold ($\sigma \approx 4.1\times10^{7}\ \mathrm{S/m}$) is plated over copper.
Estimate gold's skin depth at $1\ \mathrm{GHz}$ relative to copper's $2.1\ \mu\mathrm{m}$.
\end{exercises}
